Steps

  • Test pool volume and current sanitizer level.
  • Pick standard (about 10 ppm raise) or double for heavy algae—per label.
  • Broadcast shock with pump running; brush and circulate.
  • Retest before swimming—target safe free chlorine in range.

What This Means

Shock is a large dose of sanitizer to raise chlorine quickly and oxidize contaminants. The right amount depends on volume, starting ppm, and product strength.

Recommended Levels

  • After shock: return to 1–3 ppm free chlorine for swimming Typical
  • pH: 7.2–7.6 before/after treatment
  • Total alkalinity: 80–120 ppm (typical)

Illustrative scale: about 0.75 units of shock product per 10,000 gallons (example). Real dosing depends on current chlorine and water quality.

Common Questions

How much shock for a 5,000 gallon pool?

It depends on current water condition and target chlorine raise. Use the table on this page or the Pool Shock Calculator for exact ounces.

How much shock per 1,000 gallons?

Scale the per-pool estimates by volume—your calculator is the safest way to avoid overdosing.

Can you add too much shock?

Yes—very high chlorine can delay swimming and irritate skin. Always test and follow label safety.

How long after shocking can you swim?

Wait until free chlorine returns to a safe range per label and local guidance—often when water is clear and tests read normal.

Is liquid or granular shock better?

This page focuses on granular ounces; liquid products differ. Use the calculator and match product type to your situation.
